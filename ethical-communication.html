Early Mask Guidance & Ethical Communication

In this assignment we were tasked with reading and assessing the Surgeon General’s initial response to the COVID-19 pandemic and determining if their response was ethical. I used the IABC Code of Ethics and the reading to come to a conclusion.

According to the IABC Code of Ethics and based on what we read in chapter 1.4, I do not believe the surgeon general communicated ethically since he was aware that masks were effective and still told the general public not to buy them and going as far as to say they aren't necessary. The first thing listed in the Code of Ethics is "I am honest" and the surgeon general was knowingly dishonest when stating they were not necessary for protecting the general public. He was aware that they could save lives but chose to instead lie to save the inventory of masks for healthcare workers. In chapter 1.4, one of the main ideas is that "the ethical communicator is trustworthy" meaning that in order to effectively and ethically communicate you need trust. Openly lying to your audience is generally considered bad for trust and reputation. Trust is something that is hard to obtain but very easy to lose. In theory, the plan was to try and save the supply for those who truly needed them. However lying to the public, at the beginning of a pandemic, might have been the cause of so many people not wearing masks and the skepticism around them later on in the pandemic. It can be argued that this saved some professional lives but at the cost of civilian lives and trust in the government in a very needing time. If instead, the message was that masks should be designated to health care professionals first then the general public, the public reception of it might have been better and more ethical.
